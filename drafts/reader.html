Once upon a time there was a Piper. No one is sure where they came from or where they lived. They travelled from town to town, played beautiful music, stayed in the colorful corners of cities and towns of the coast, and loved whomever they wished. It was healing. They learned, in the ragged hollows, tricks of the trade. They learned to lure away the rats from their granaries with songs and to play music that would speak to the sparks within each other, finding those who were hollowed out, rejected, and broken — calling them to the cracks between the streets, the places where the rats were tame and food stores managed with care.
And then the plagues began.

One by one the piper watched their friends fall. The massive trade ships from across the sea brought rats who were not cared for among people who were pressed to work, and the stress and compression of it all became sickness that spread through the ports first, then inland.

The piper helped where they could but soon their community was whittled away — often taken from the streets by the ratcatchers themselves because they were assumed to be part of the problem.
